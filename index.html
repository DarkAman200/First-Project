Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah, often referred to as the "Father of the Nation" in Pakistan, was born on December 25, 1876, in Karachi, then part of British India. Jinnah played a pivotal role in the creation of Pakistan and is widely acknowledged for his leadership in achieving independence for the country in 1947. Trained as a barrister in London, Jinnah initially pursued a successful career in law before becoming actively involved in Indian politics. He joined the Indian National Congress but later became disillusioned with its approach, particularly regarding the representation of Muslim interests. This led him to advocate for the establishment of a separate Muslim state, which eventually resulted in the creation of Pakistan. Jinnah was a highly articulate and principled leader who played a key role in the formation of the All-India Muslim League in 1906. Over the years, he became a staunch advocate for the rights of Muslims in a united India. His efforts culminated in the demand for a separate nation for Muslims, which was realized with the creation of Pakistan on August 14, 1947. Quaid-e-Azam Jinnah became Pakistan's first Governor-General until his death on September 11, 1948. His vision for Pakistan was one of a modern, democratic state that would provide equal opportunities for all citizens regardless of their religious or ethnic background. Jinnah's legacy continues to shape Pakistan's identity, and he is revered for his unwavering commitment to the ideals of justice, equality, and self-determination.

His poetry has had a profound impact on the Urdu language and has inspired many people, both within and beyond the borders of Pakistan. Iqbal was a strong advocate for the political and cultural revival of Islamic civilization. He played a significant role in the intellectual and political spheres of his time, and his ideas had a lasting impact on the formation of Pakistan. His poetry encourages individuals to strive for self-realization, moral integrity, and the pursuit of knowledge. In addition to his literary contributions, Iqbal was involved in politics and served as a member of the legislative assembly in British India. Allama Iqbal's legacy continues to be celebrated in Pakistan and among Urdu speakers worldwide, and his poetry remains an influential source of inspiration for those interested in philosophy, spirituality, and the cultural heritage of South Asia.
